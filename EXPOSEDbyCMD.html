Koch Employee Could Become Next Governor of Kansas

By Don Wiener
 | 
July 15th, 2026
 
at 11:12 AM (CDT)

Arguably, no state legislator has stronger ties to Charles Koch’s political network than Ty Masterson (R-16), president of the Kansas state Senate. He has chaired a key Koch-funded policy and advocacy organization, has been endorsed by the Koch network’s political operation, and collects a salary of $217,000 from a nonprofit center at Wichita State University (WSU) funded by the Koch Foundation, an offshoot of the Wichita-based Koch Inc.

With President Trump’s endorsement, Masterson is favored to win the Republican primary for Kansas governor on August 4.

Americans for Prosperity (AFP), Koch’s right-wing astroturf operation, has also endorsed Masterson in the primary.

AFP, which is registered as a 501(c)(4) nonprofit, secures the majority of its funding from the Koch-controlled Stand Together Chamber of Commerce. Its super PAC, AFP Action, runs independent expenditure electoral campaigns.

In announcing its endorsement, AFP Regional Director Elizabeth Patton said, “This year’s election is a critical opportunity to elect a principled conservative leader to the governor’s mansion after eight years of constant blockades by progressive Governor Laura Kelly that held our state back from its full potential.”

Masterson chaired the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC) in 2024 and has served on its board of directors since 2021. This pay-to-play operation brings together state legislators, corporate lobbyists, and far-right operatives behind closed doors to write model legislation that advances a radical right-wing, pro-corporate, and pro-Republican agenda on everything from gerrymandering and purported election fraud to crushing unions, undermining public education, and rewriting the Constitution.

Koch’s donor conduits are major funders of ALEC, and for decades its lead lobbyist, Michael Morgan, has served on ALEC’s Private Enterprise Advisory Council. Charles Koch may also give to the organization via personal checks or through Koch Inc. or Koch Companies Public Sector, though there is no way to confirm or track these donations.

Masterson’s ties to the Koch network go beyond his ALEC leadership role and the AFP endorsement. Since a creative makerspace at WSU opened in 2017, he has held the position of director of the “officially branded ‘GoCreate, a Koch Collaborative,’” according to the Kansas City Star, where students and paying community members can access specialized tools and studio spaces to design and make their own work. The paper also reports that in the decade since, WSU has paid Masterson $1.1 million to run the center, even though it’s unclear what, if anything, he does there.

A recent editorial in the Kansas Reflector called on Masterson “to tell voters clearly and forcefully how many hours per week he works as director, how frequently he shows up to his campus office, and what tangible results he has produced. He needs to say what he actually does.”

Masterson on the Issues

In the 16 years since Congress passed the Affordable Care Act (ACA), states have been encouraged to expand their Medicaid programs with federal support, which covers more than 90% of the cost of providing healthcare coverage to more low-income residents.

As chair of ALEC, Masterson was a national spokesperson for the organization’s ongoing opposition to Medicaid expansion. He also played a major role in preventing the Kansas Medicaid program from enrolling more of the state’s working poor residents.

In 2025, Governor Kelly (D) sought that approval when she included Medicaid expansion in her proposed budget for this year. It directed the state to expand Medicaid by January 2026 and tap into additional federal Medicaid matching funds made available by the American Rescue Plan of 2021 (ARP), which offers an additional 5% in the federal matching rate for two years after adoption for holdout states that adopt expansion.

Since becoming governor in 2019, Kelly has proposed Medicaid expansion in previous budgets and legislation, but so far her requests have failed to pass in the Republican-controlled legislature.

As for the governor’s repeated attempts to expand Medicaid coverage, Masterson spouted the Trump administration’s unfounded claims of fraud in the program and boasted that he “100% believes” in keeping Kansas from expanding healthcare coverage for its low-income residents. The stalled expansion leaves an estimated 150,000 Kansans in a “coverage gap,” meaning they earn too much to qualify for traditional Medicaid but too little to afford private insurance.

This year Masterson supported a property tax plan that would have capped the amount local governments could spend from revenue collected through property taxes. Cities and counties would have been required to keep spending increases below 3% or the national inflation rate, whichever was higher. If local governments proposed spending too much, residents could have signed a petition to block the budget.

Democratic legislators opposed the plan, and the governor vetoed it. The main objection was that local governments would have to raise sales and excise taxes to offset the lost revenue, so there would be no real reduction in the taxes collected. Democrats argued that sales taxes are regressive since they take higher amounts from the poor relative to their income.

Other proposals backed by Masterson that died during the 2026 session (which is now in recess until January 2027) included SB 302, an act to keep students from using “personal electronic communication devices” in public schools and school officials from using social media to disseminate official school information.

Masterson was only one of many state senators who sponsored the bill, which uses language from an ALEC model bill on Limiting Student Use of Cell Phones in K–12 Classrooms.

A Center for Media and Democracy analysis found that even though Masterson shepherded plenty of GOP legislation through the state legislature as Senate president, the body did not pass a single major piece of legislation for which he was the original sponsor.
